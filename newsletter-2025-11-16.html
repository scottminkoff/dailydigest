US News and Politics

President Trump announced he was cutting ties with Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene, calling her a "ranting lunatic" on Truth Social and signaling support for a primary challenge. The break followed Greene's recent calls for the release of the Justice Department’s files on Jeffrey Epstein and criticism of the president's handling of the matter. Greene responded on X by posting screenshots of her texts to Trump and labeling his attack as an effort to "scare all the other Republicans." The House is expected to vote on Tuesday on bipartisan legislation that would force the full release of all Epstein-related records from the DOJ. In a related move, President Trump ordered the Justice Department to investigate ties between Epstein and prominent Democrats, assigning Manhattan U.S. Attorney Jay Clayton to the probe.

Following the end of the government shutdown, the Department of Transportation is lowering flight cuts at 40 major airports to three percent, reflecting improvements in air traffic controller staffing. Funding for the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) is now flowing again to recipients after a period described by officials as a "nail-biting scramble." However, funding for the Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP), which helps with winter heating bills, could take weeks to reopen.

The Heritage Foundation is experiencing internal turmoil after its president, Kevin Roberts, defended Tucker Carlson's recent friendly interview with white nationalist Nick Fuentes. In an all-hands meeting, some Heritage scholars expressed outrage, with one calling Carlson's show a "lunatic asylum." The controversy has highlighted a broader divide on the right, with Vice President JD Vance remaining quiet on the issue. According to The New York Times, Vance is seen as "groyper adjacent," and Fuentes has interpreted his silence as a political victory, suggesting the Vice President fears the consequences of a disavowal.

A New York Times investigation found that the Trump administration has transformed the Department of Homeland Security to carry out its mass deportation campaign, diverting resources from efforts to combat child predators, sex trafficking, and black market sales. As part of a new crackdown, Border Patrol agents began arresting people across Charlotte, N.C., this weekend. The agency's recruiting materials are also reportedly taking on a nativist tone, with one official stating that a key task is "defending our culture and what it means to be an American."

In other political news, a Federal Reserve governor who stepped down in August was found to have repeatedly violated the central bank’s stock trading rules, according to a federal ethics report. In Indiana, the Republican-led state Senate has declared it will not convene to redraw congressional maps, a significant blow to the White House's efforts to shore up the GOP House majority through redistricting.

New York State and New York City News and Politics

In the ongoing race for New York City Council speaker, the Brooklyn delegation, the largest in the council, is attempting to position itself as a unified voting bloc. According to City & State, Brooklyn Democratic Party Leader Rodneyse Bichotte Hermelyn and other members are working to consolidate their influence to avoid getting the "short end of the stick" as they have in past elections. The front-runners are seen as Manhattan's Julie Menin and Brooklyn's Crystal Hudson, with Amanda Farías as a potential compromise candidate.

A judge has temporarily ordered New York City to refrain from entering into a new agreement with Mets owner Steve Cohen regarding the use of the parking lot at Citi Field. The move could potentially torpedo Cohen's bid to develop a casino in the area.

The Trump administration is exploring the use of a Coast Guard facility on Staten Island to hold detained immigrants as it escalates immigration roundups in the city. Separately, the use of county jails in New York by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement has surged this year as part of President Trump's broader deportation agenda.

Brooklyn Hospital Center is at risk of closure by the end of the year if it does not receive financial assistance from the state. Elected officials are sounding the alarm and urging Gov. Kathy Hochul to intervene to prevent a shutdown.

Conservationists are again urging Gov. Hochul to approve a ban on the harvesting of horseshoe crabs in state waters. The governor vetoed the same measure a year ago. In other state news, a federal judge has thrown out a Trump administration lawsuit against the city of Rochester over its sanctuary city policy.

International News and Politics

In an interview with The Bulwark, writer and surgeon Dr. Atul Gawande discussed the devastating human toll of the Trump administration's decision to halt foreign aid and dismantle the U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID). Gawande, a former Biden administration official, stated that the cuts have already led to an estimated 600,000 deaths, with 400,000 of them being children. A new documentary he produced with The New Yorker, "Rovina's Choice," chronicles the consequences of these policies by following a Sudanese refugee in Kenya struggling to get aid for her malnourished child. Gawande noted that modern food assistance programs had previously dropped mortality rates from severe malnutrition from over 20% to under 5%.

President Trump told reporters that he has "sort of" decided how to move forward on Venezuela but did not offer specifics. His comments came after several days of high-level meetings where officials discussed possible military operations in the country. A classified DOJ brief reportedly describes fentanyl from the region as a potential chemical weapons threat.

Russia launched a major drone and missile attack on Kyiv, killing at least six people. Ukrainian officials said the assault targeted the country's power grid as winter approaches, in an effort to plunge the nation into cold and darkness.

According to The New York Times, The Trump Organization is in talks to bring a Trump-branded property to a major real estate development in Saudi Arabia overseen by the government and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, who is scheduled to visit Washington next week. The project aims to transform the historic town of Diriyah into a luxury destination.

Survivors are beginning to speak out about the trauma of being seized as leverage by the regime of former Syrian dictator Bashar al-Assad. During the conflict, the wives and children of rebels were systematically taken to pressure opposition fighters.

Culture and Technology

According to a New York Times report, a growing number of patients are turning to A.I. chatbots like ChatGPT for medical advice. Users cited long wait times, inattentive doctors, and high costs as reasons for seeking an alternative. Many reported that the bots offer an accessible and empathetic "bedside manner" that they feel is lacking in the traditional healthcare system. While some doctors acknowledged the system's flaws, they also expressed concern that patients are relying on a technology known to provide incomplete or fabricated answers for high-stakes health decisions.

Scientists have successfully sequenced RNA from the 39,000-year-old remains of a woolly mammoth. The genetic material was sourced from a well-preserved mummy known as Yuka, making it the oldest RNA ever recorded by approximately 25,000 years. The breakthrough, detailed in the journal Cell, also revealed a surprise: genetic analysis of the Y chromosome showed that Yuka, long believed to be female, was actually male.

The American penny was officially discontinued this week after 232 years of production.

Guillermo del Toro's new adaptation of Mary Shelley's "Frankenstein" is now available on Netflix. A review in The Atlantic describes the film as a "handsome, burnished production" with the "deep well of compassion that runs through all of del Toro’s work," but questions if it feels "inert."

The driverless taxi industry in San Francisco is facing new scrutiny after one of the vehicles killed a beloved cat.
